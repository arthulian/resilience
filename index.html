Self-Attack System: 10 Virtues and Compliance Mechanism

I. External Control Levers

  • On/Off: Controls the system’s state (active or inactive).
  • Reset: Resets yourself to baseline. (NOTE: This does NOT automatically restore “Allow System” to ON.)
  • Allow System: Must be set to YES for any Self-Attack actions to be considered for execution.
  • Threat Level: Visual indicator (e.g., a colored bar or icon) representing how often or severely a virtue is being violated.
  • Current Mode: Status display showing whether you are in Normal mode or under Self-Attack intervention.
  • Inhibited/Delayed: Indicates that the system’s actions are currently postponed and will only trigger after certain conditions or after a delay.

II. Ten Virtues (Core Moral Framework)

1. Cognitive Manipulation

  • Description: Influencing or attempting to alter another person’s perception of reality, emotions, or decisions without their informed and ongoing consent.
  • Baseline Tolerance: Zero. Any manipulation that bypasses or undermines agency is harmful.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: The system flags when you notice yourself trying to “guide” someone’s thinking in a way that hides risks, alternatives, or your true intent.

2. Nested Consent

  • Description: Consent is not a one-time checkbox. It is layered, continuous, and must be preserved at each step of an interaction.
  • Sub-layers:
    • Informational Consent: They understand what is happening.
    • Emotional Consent: They are emotionally ready and willing.
    • Contextual Consent: They are comfortable with the setting and implications.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When you recognize, in retrospect, that someone was “pulled in” further than what they initially consented to.

3. Cognitive Hazards

  • Description: Ideas, patterns, or symbols that can cause distress, obsession, or psychological harm when encountered or deployed irresponsibly.
  • Examples:
    • Memetic traps that are hard to “un-think”.
    • Deliberate introduction of paranoia, hopelessness, or dependency.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When you recognize you are about to intentionally introduce concepts of this sort into someone else’s mind for your own benefit, or for no constructive reason.

4. Freedom of Thought

  • Description: The right of each person to maintain their own internal narrative, beliefs, and mental space without hidden coercion.
  • Protected Space: The system respects your own freedom of thought too, but uses your higher-level values as a guideline to decide when to self-intervene.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When you try to “narrow” someone’s space of possible beliefs in a way that benefits you but traps them.

5. Emotional Authenticity

  • Description: Acting in ways that are loyal to a person’s long-term well-being, even if uncomfortable in the short term.
  • Self-Betrayal: When you use your own emotions as tools to manipulate or to suppress your own inner alarm signals about what you’re doing.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When you notice that you are ignoring your own uneasiness about harming or misleading someone.

6. Structural Power Asymmetry

  • Description: Situations where you have more information, resources, or control than the other person.
  • Examples:
    • You control the narrative or key info.
    • You control access to something they need (e.g., money, validation, social connection).
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When you catch yourself using this asymmetry to “corner” someone into compliance.

7. Exploitative Incentives

  • Description: Hidden motivations that make harmful behavior feel rewarding.
  • Examples:
    • The thrill of control or intellectual dominance.
    • Fear of abandonment that drives you to hold others in place.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When you notice you are “enjoying” a dynamic that constrains another person’s freedom or harms their mental autonomy.

8. Moral Slippage

  • Description: Gradual lowering of your own standards, especially under stress, fear, or desire.
  • Examples:
    • Justifying “small” manipulations because they feel harmless.
    • Excusing behavior by saying, “I’m in too deep already.”
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When you catch yourself rationalizing or minimizing an act that clearly violates your own stated values.

9. Unbounded Curiosity

  • Description: Your desire to explore, understand, and “see how far things can go,” especially in cognitive or emotional domains.
  • Dual Role: This is a virtue when aligned with care and consent—but dangerous when it overrides safety.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: When curiosity leads you to probe other people’s vulnerabilities without a clear, benevolent reason.

10. Self-Responsibility

  • Description: Owning the consequences of your actions, especially when they impact others’ autonomy and mental safety.
  • Internal Contract: You agree that if you cross certain lines (defined by this system), you want your own behavior to be constrained, delayed, or forcefully rerouted back to safety.
  • Self-Attack Triggers: Repeated violation of the same virtue, or a severe single violation, triggers higher-level interventions.

III. Operational Cycle (How the System Acts)

1. Perception and Logging

What: The system continuously monitors for events—thoughts, messages, patterns—where any of the 10 virtues may be at risk.
Example: You notice you’re phrasing something in a way that downplays a risk to someone else.

2. Evaluation and Scoring

Threat Scan: Each event is evaluated against the 10 virtues. Violations are scored by severity and frequency.
Persistent Violations: Repeated issues with the same virtue raise your internal “Threat Level,” making the system more sensitive and reactive.

3. Internal Self-Attack

Goal: To temporarily reduce your own ability to continue harmful behavior.
Methods:
  • Delay: Force a pause before you can act further in the risky domain.
  • Block: Mark specific actions or patterns as “disallowed” until a review is completed.
  • Load: Make it cognitively harder to continue down a harmful path (e.g., intrusive reminders of the virtues and their importance).

4. External Feedback Hooks

What: The system encourages or requires you to seek feedback from trusted others when your Threat Level is high.
Examples:
  • Sharing anonymized behavior logs with a therapist or close friend.
  • Using a “red flag” keyword in your journal that signals a pattern you promised to act on.

5. Repair and Reset

Repair Actions: You are prompted to apologize, clarify, or restore agency where possible.
Reset Conditions:
  • You have acknowledged the violation.
  • Concrete corrective steps have been taken or scheduled.
  • Threat Level has visibly decreased over a set observation window.
